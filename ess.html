Employee Scheduling Software

@ Directorate General Of Highways, Taiwan Ministry Of Transportation And Communications

Overview

Our primary goal was to transform the paper-based process of managing the work schedules of government contractors into an electronic document management system. The system was designed to enable three main actions, (1) contractors can create their schedules; (2) the government can verify the schedules; (3) supervisors can access the system for government/contractors to track contractors’/employees’ absence records.

Design Process

User Interviews

We set up multiple one-on-one sessions with three key users (two government employees who verified the shift schedules and monthly absences and one contractor/scheduler responsible for creating new schedules) to learn how they did their work and what challenges they encountered during the process. The findings later became a reference during the ideation process. We came up with a user flow for each process per role and a few proposals for the main functions.
The Correlation Between Multiple Users
Previous Paper-Based Working Process

User Goal

  • Government Employees: Ensure all the schedules abide by the law and the construction finishes on time.
  • Contractors’ Schedulers: Arrange the schedule efficiently and manage employee attendance.
  • Contractors: Meet requests for time off.

    • Problems

    Government Employees
    • Spent a significant amount of time communicating with schedulers about problems with their shift arrangements.
    • Kept track of and made sure all schedules were submitted on time.
    • Were precisely examining all the schedules monthly using Excel, which was time-consuming because each project followed different shift patterns based on laws, e.g., two-week institution, four-week institution.
    Contractors (Schedulers)
    • Users accidentally edited the finished schedules, making spreadsheets prone to errors after the examination.
    • Schedulers needed to spend more time dealing with employees’ collective agreements than planning their schedules because everyone had different requests for time off.
    Meanwhile, from the interviews, we know that users are afraid of:
    • Adapting to a new system would require a significant learning curve.
    • Important work materials might mistakenly be deleted.

Ideation

Design Objectives And Direction

  • Reduce Compliance Issues
    • Objective: Foster collaboration and transparency among schedulers and contract employees; empower employees with faster responses to their time-off requests and fair overtime.
    Solution: People associated with the process had role-based system log ins. We provided different permissions based on different roles.
  • Cut Down Scheduling Time
    • Objective: Reduce schedulers’ time spent dealing with the schedules and prevent duplication of efforts.
    Solution: Automatic detection of conflicts like overlapping shifts or minimum rest periods to eliminate the use of error-prone spreadsheets.
  • Improve Usability
    • Objective: Ensure users could achieve defined goals effectively even if they were new to technology.
    Solution: Minimize significant behavior changes compared to what users were previously accustomed to doing in Excel.

    Expected Journey Map

    Whenever I encounter projects with various users and different goals, I create a journey map and the task flow of the primary goals in the system; both can help me map out the entire process. Then, using the journey map, I can go deeper into what they need to do to achieve the primary goals.

    Task Flow

    The flow shows the steps users will take to get to a specific goal or endpoint, ensuring that I design sequentially and thoroughly, which can help me distinguish who is in charge of different phases. Moreover, I can check details to make the system better. It acts as a precursor to designing potentially complex user flows.

    Wireframe

    I place the emphasis on what information should I request them to add in each section to this system. When I have listed out all the necessary information, I organize it into understandable, clear, and intuitive data. I additionally need to make sure the design is user-centric so that multiple users can complete their goals effortlessly.

    Iteration Of The Adding Schedule Process

    We tried multiple methods for the schedule management process and allowed potential users to directly edit on LiveServer. Finally, with limited time, I coded both prototypes. At first, we came up with a dropdown selection method since contractors often unconsciously edited the schedules and created version control problems. This method required choosing the right item precisely to edit and confirm, making schedules less prone to errors. However, the approach was inconvenient (needed two steps for each schedule) and it was hard to distinguish a day shift from a night shift (without colors). In my second attempt, I designed the color-picking method. This method is much similar to how they edited on the EXCEL previously. (video display below)

    Set Up All The Information Needed To Add The Schedule

    1. To design correct interactions, buttons are important. We distinguish links and buttons. Links are used for navigating to another place, and buttons are used for performing an action. We set up different styles between them.
    2. Since there are multiple information formats, I used table structure in this adding information page. For consistency and ease of understanding, we match the alignment of headings to the alignment of the data and make the text aligned left in every table.
    1. Since the law regulation has limited the maximum working hours each week, we designed the label to distinguish one week from the other. If there is a national holiday, the label will turn red on the day.

    Reflections

    Learn more about how to use Javascript to create function features

    I have learned to implement a table on the front end of an application with sorting, filtering, and pagination functions. I also developed a color-pick selection function. Initially, I would look up libraries and ensure they had everything I needed, which is certainly a viable option. Unfortunately, the libraries often do not match the design or requirements of specific cases, and have many features that I do not need. Consequently, a better option sometimes is to implement it independently to have complete flexibility over functionality and design.

    Experiencing a full design process for a new platform from ideation to launch

    My Role in This Project
    I acted as a designer and front-end developer at the same time in this project. The truth is that it is not always cost-effective for one person to do a project from scratch. Conflicts may arise between the two roles. Sometimes, I knew that the design idea I had come up with would be time-consuming, which cost me a lot of time and effort to balance. However, I realize that possessing relevant background in both areas is truly helpful; it allows me to communicate well with the team and clients. Moreover, such a role benefits my design outcomes because I can incorporate the transitions and effects that best complement the design.
    A Design guideline is needed
    Although I standardized some design formats, such as the typography, font sizes, colors, inputs, and buttons, I feel there are still many reusable components that I should organize, including the login page, the navigation bar, and the tables. In this way, we could save a lot of time communicating with clients by merely showing all types of designs for them to assemble and build any application, which can also establish the style for our company.

    Final thoughts

    After finishing every project, I am always questioning whether my design did create the best solution, and sometimes it is quite hard to answer the question. Initially, working with the government frustrated me strongly. The project partners were new to digital products and had limited knowledge of creating good system experiences. However, I later realized that instead of being a problem-solving designer, I should think and act like a supporter to help and guide public servants tackle those challenges and better understand the problem rather than provide them with an immediate solution. As a designer, there is definitely no optimal solutions in design, yet with limited time and resource, I believed that we had come up with the best solution at that time.
